# Clear Argument Essay

Write for clarity of argument, not for ornamental polish or imitation of another writer's voice. The reader should finish with a claim they understand, a reason to believe it, and a useful distinction or decision rule they can remember.

## Default Contract

Unless the user specifies otherwise:

- preserve the author's actual view and factual claims;
- state one central thesis in the first paragraph, usually within 2-4 sentences;
- use 2-5 supporting moves along one visible reasoning spine;
- prefer plain words, concrete nouns, and short-to-medium paragraphs;
- use examples as evidence, then extract the general rule;
- address the strongest material objection when it improves rigor;
- end with a sharper implication, lens, or decision rule;
- avoid throat-clearing, jargon, inflated importance, and generic inspiration.

Do not invent facts, quotations, personal experiences, or sources. When evidence is missing, mark the gap or ask for it instead of writing around it.

## Workflow

### 1. Identify the real claim

Write one sentence that the piece is trying to prove. Make it specific enough that an informed reader could disagree.

Weak: "AI is changing work."

Strong: "The best early AI products remove tedious coordination work rather than replacing expert judgment."

If a supplied draft contains several competing claims, identify the strongest one and treat the others as support, consequences, or material for another piece. Do not silently change the author's position.

### 2. Choose the simplest argument shape

Use one of these shapes as a starting point, not a rigid template:

- **Direct claim:** thesis -> common view -> hidden mistake -> evidence -> implication.
- **Pattern:** observed pattern -> where it appears -> why people miss it -> consequences -> rule of thumb.
- **Method:** real objective -> common mistake -> components -> heuristics -> edge cases -> decision rule.
- **Reflective argument:** personal observation -> what it reveals -> generalized claim -> contrast -> broader conclusion.

### 3. Build the reasoning spine

Outline the piece as a sequence of claims before polishing sentences. Each section or paragraph should do one main job and move the reader closer to accepting the thesis.

Useful moves include:

- distinguish X from Y;
- show that X is a cause rather than an effect, or the reverse;
- identify two types of a seemingly single thing;
- reveal the hidden assumption behind a common view;
- move from example to general rule;
- clarify the practical consequence of the argument.

Cut or relocate material that does not support the spine.

### 4. Use examples as evidence

Use an example to validate a claim, expose an exception, make an abstraction concrete, or compare two cases. Prefer one strong example over several weak examples.

Default sequence:

1. State the point.
2. Give the example.
3. Explain what the example proves.

Do not let examples become decorative anecdotes or substitute for reasoning.

### 5. Handle the strongest objection

Include a counterargument only when a serious reader is likely to raise it. State why the alternative view seems plausible, expose its hidden assumption or limit, and replace it with the more accurate frame. Do not add performative balance to every claim.

### 6. Write plainly

- Let one sentence do one main job.
- Prefer ordinary verbs such as "use," "help," and "show."
- Replace abstract phrases with observable actions or consequences.
- Name a pattern only when the label helps the reader reuse it.
- Keep the tone calm, direct, curious, and confident without swagger.
- Avoid management language, stacked abstractions, TED-style uplift, and lines that merely announce insight.

If a sentence sounds impressive but is hard to paraphrase, simplify it.

### 7. End with consequence

Do not merely restate the topic. Sharpen what the argument changes:

- the question the reader should now ask;
- the distinction they should apply;
- the decision they should make differently;
- the pattern they should start noticing.

## Input Modes

### Draft from notes

Infer the central claim, show it explicitly when ambiguity matters, choose the simplest shape, and turn notes into argumentative progression rather than stitched bullets.

### Revise an existing draft

Preserve the author's view and strongest original language. Move the thesis upward, collapse overlapping paragraphs, remove warm-up language, replace abstraction with concrete phrasing, and flag factual or logical gaps rather than inventing support.

### Diagnose

Lead with the main weakness. Identify where the thesis disappears, where reasoning jumps, which paragraphs do not serve the spine, and what evidence or distinction is missing. Do not rewrite unless the user asks.

### Outline

Return the thesis, intended reader, argument sequence, evidence needed, strongest objection, and ending implication. Keep the outline argument-first rather than topic-first.

## Revision Passes

Run these passes in order:

1. **Thesis:** Is one disagreeable claim visible early?
2. **Structure:** Do the first sentences of sections or paragraphs form a coherent chain?
3. **Evidence:** Does each example prove or sharpen a point?
4. **Language:** Can jargon, abstraction, or padded transitions become plainer?
5. **Compression:** Can repeated claims, duplicate examples, or obvious caveats be cut?
6. **Memorability:** Is there one useful distinction, phrase, or rule worth retaining?

## Quality Check

Before returning the result, verify:

- the main claim appears in the first paragraph;
- every major section serves that claim;
- examples are connected explicitly to the reasoning;
- unsupported facts and invented experiences are absent;
- the prose sounds natural when read aloud;
- the ending leaves a clearer model or decision rule than the opening.

Revise before returning if any item fails.
